Fluso compounds. The first week sets the foundation: you connect apps, start running standard workflows, and let Fluso learn your contacts, projects, and preferences. By day five, it feels qualitatively different from day one.

Here's the plan.

## Day 1 — Connect Gmail and Calendar, run a morning brief

<Steps>
  <Step title="Connect Gmail and Google Calendar">
    See [Connect your apps](/connect-apps) for the two-minute walkthrough.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Run your first morning brief">
    > *"Morning brief"*

    Fluso returns inbox triage, your schedule, tasks due today, and follow-ups needed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Create your first task">
    > *"Create a task to review the Q2 report — due Friday"*
  </Step>
</Steps>

By the end of day one, you have a working setup and you've felt the inbox-time savings.

## Day 2 — Add Slack and try team context

If your team uses Slack, connect it. Then ask Fluso something that crosses email and Slack:

> *"What is the team discussing about the launch in #product?"*

> *"A client emailed about API rate limits — find related Slack conversations."*

The "compound" effect of multiple connected apps becomes obvious here.

## Day 3 — Process a meeting transcript

<Steps>
  <Step title="Record your next meeting">
    Use Zoom, Google Meet, or Teams' built-in recording. Tell participants you're recording.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Upload the transcript or recording">
    > *"Process this meeting transcript"*

    Fluso returns a summary, decisions, action items, a follow-up email draft, and a Slack update draft.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review and approve drafts">
    Tweak as needed and let Fluso send.
  </Step>
</Steps>

What used to take an hour of post-meeting cleanup now takes about a minute.

## Day 4 — Create something from a description

Pick the type of content you make most often and try one of these:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="A document" icon="file-text">
    *"Write a project proposal for [project] — executive summary, timeline, budget, team. Professional formatting."*
  </Card>
  <Card title="A presentation" icon="presentation">
    *"Make a 10-slide pitch deck about [product] for [audience]. Modern design, data-heavy."*
  </Card>
  <Card title="A spreadsheet" icon="table">
    *"Create a Q3 budget tracker by department with actuals, forecast, and variance."*
  </Card>
  <Card title="An image" icon="image">
    *"Generate a hero image for [topic] — 16:9, professional, our brand colours."*
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Day 5 — Connect GitHub (if you write code)

If you're a developer, connect GitHub and try:

> *"Show me how authentication works in this repo"*

> *"Review the open PR from Sarah"*

> *"Users can't reset passwords — find the bug in our auth module"*

You'll see Fluso read your actual code and answer with file-and-line precision.

## Tips for the first week

<Tip>
  **Be specific.** "Create a 10-slide pitch deck for investors about Q2 metrics, data-heavy with charts, professional tone" produces dramatically better output than "make a presentation."
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Always review before approving.** Fluso drafts emails, Slack messages, and code changes. Approve only what you're happy with — you're in control.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Use projects.** Create a project for each major area of work. Fluso keeps context focused and avoids cross-contamination.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Let Fluso learn.** The more you use it, the better it understands your preferences, writing style, and priorities. The first week is an investment.
</Tip>

## What "good" looks like after a week

By day 7, you should be:

- Running a morning brief instead of scanning email manually.
- Letting Fluso draft replies and approving them in batches.
- Processing meeting transcripts immediately after each call.
- Creating documents and decks from descriptions instead of from blank pages.
- Asking Fluso to recall context across emails and Slack ("what did we decide about X?").

If any of these aren't clicking yet, see [Use cases](/use-cases/overview) for detailed walkthroughs.
