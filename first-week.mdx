Fluso gets noticeably more useful as it learns your contacts, your projects, and your house style. The first week is when most of that learning happens. The plan below is what works for most people; treat it as a guide, not a checklist.

## Day one

Connect Gmail and Calendar. Run a morning brief:

> *"Morning brief"*

You'll get inbox triage, today's schedule, what's due, and any follow-ups that need a nudge. Skim it, approve any drafts you like, and that's the first habit installed.

Before lunch, create one task by talking to Fluso instead of typing into a task manager:

> *"Create a task to review the Q2 report. Due Friday."*

The point isn't the task — it's noticing how short the loop is.

## Day two

If your team uses Slack, connect it. Then ask something that crosses two integrations:

> *"What is the team saying about the launch in #product?"*

> *"A client emailed about API rate limits. Pull related Slack discussions and draft a response."*

This is when the "lives inside your tools" framing stops being marketing and starts being obvious.

## Day three

Process a meeting. Record your next call (Zoom, Meet, or Teams will do this for you; tell the participants). After the meeting:

> *"Process this meeting transcript."*

You get a summary, the decisions, action items as tasks, a follow-up email draft, and a Slack update if relevant. Two minutes to review beats an hour of post-meeting cleanup.

Do this once and you'll do it every meeting after.

## Day four

Make something from a description. Pick the kind of artifact you usually start from a blank page and try one of these:

> *"Write a project proposal for [project]. Executive summary, timeline, budget, team. Professional tone."*

> *"Make a 10-slide pitch deck about [product] for [audience]. Modern, data-heavy."*

> *"Create a Q3 budget tracker by department. Actuals, forecast, variance."*

> *"Generate a hero image for [topic]. 16:9, professional, our brand colours."*

The first version isn't always perfect, but you'll spend ten minutes editing instead of an hour starting.

## Day five

If you write code, connect GitHub. Try a few things on a repo you know well:

> *"Show me how authentication works in this repo."*

> *"Review the open PR from Sarah."*

> *"Users can't reset passwords. Find the bug in our auth module."*

The point of these prompts is to see Fluso reading your actual code, not generic patterns. The answers come back with file-and-line references.

## Through the week

A few things to watch for as the week goes:

The morning brief gets better. By Wednesday, the noise filter is calibrated to your inbox; FYI messages stop sneaking into the action list.

Auto-tasks settle. Day one might over-create. Day three is roughly right. Day five it's quiet on stuff that doesn't need attention.

Drafts start to sound like you. The first replies will be a bit generic. By a few days in, Fluso has read enough of your sent mail to match tone.

If something feels off, say so out loud. *"More direct,"* *"shorter,"* *"don't use exclamation marks"* — these stick.

## What good looks like by Friday

You aren't opening Gmail in the morning anymore. The brief is enough.

You're batch-approving drafts at the start of the day instead of writing them.

Your task list builds itself; manual entry has become rare.

Meeting prep is one prompt, post-meeting cleanup is one prompt, and you do both reliably.

You've started a project for whatever you're focused on this week, and Fluso scopes its answers to it.

If two or three of those aren't clicking, [Use cases](/use-cases/overview) shows them in detail with the specific prompts.

## Things that pay off later

Add `context.md` to a project's workspace if you have conventions worth writing down — a tone of voice, a tech stack, do-not-touch zones. Fluso reads it and follows.

Process meeting transcripts even when you took notes. The graph builds from them.

Disconnect anything you don't actually use. One quiet integration is better than three forgotten ones.
