Fluso is an assistant that lives inside the apps you already use. You connect Gmail. You connect your calendar. Maybe Slack, maybe GitHub. After that, you can ask Fluso to do almost anything you'd otherwise do yourself in those tools, in plain English, and it does it.

It is not a chat window with a model behind it. It reads your inbox the way you read your inbox. It puts events on your calendar. It writes a 12-slide deck and hands you the file. It opens a pull request when you ask it to. The difference matters because the time savings only show up when the assistant can act.

## The work it takes off your plate

The shape of a normal week, before:

- An hour a day on email triage.
- Twenty minutes finding a meeting time and sending the invite.
- Forty minutes of notes and follow-ups after a meeting that ran an hour.
- A morning gone to writing a deck from a blank slide.
- An afternoon researching across fifteen tabs and trying to pull it together.
- Constant tab-switching to remember what you and someone else last said about something.

Fluso handles each of these. None of them perfectly the first time, but well enough that you stop doing them yourself.

## What it does, by example

Instead of scanning fifty unread emails: you ask for a summary, and you get a list of what needs action, what's FYI, and what's noise. The action items become tracked tasks, with deadlines and links back to the source.

Instead of staring at a blank Google Doc: you describe what you want — a project proposal with executive summary, timeline, and budget — and a formatted doc is in chat thirty seconds later.

Instead of digging through three months of Slack: you ask what the team decided about pricing and Fluso quotes the message and links you to it.

Instead of reading a two-hour transcript: you upload it, and you get a summary, the decisions, the action items as tasks with assignees, a follow-up email draft, and a Slack update draft. Reviewing all of that takes about two minutes.

Instead of opening fifteen tabs: you ask for a competitive analysis of five enterprise platforms with citations, and you get a research report and a twelve-slide deck.

The pattern is the same: describe the outcome, review what comes back, approve or edit.

## Things you can make from a description

Documents, decks, spreadsheets with formulas and charts, images, flowcharts, web pages — landing pages and dashboards as deployable code if you want them. Fluso writes the first draft. You take it from there.

## Code

Connect GitHub and Fluso reads your repos. Ask how authentication works and you get an answer with file-and-line references, not a generic explanation. Ask it to review an open PR and you get a real review: bugs, missing tests, security concerns. Describe a bug and Fluso traces the code, finds the cause, writes the fix, and opens the PR. You approve the diff before anything is merged.

## Memory

Every email, every meeting transcript, every Slack thread, every doc — Fluso quietly builds a graph from them. People you talk to, projects you work on, decisions you've made. Six weeks later, when someone mentions a company you researched in January, you can ask "what do I know about Company X?" and get the actual answer, not your vague recollection of it.

The graph compounds. Week one it knows your frequent contacts and current projects. By month three it surfaces patterns you'd miss yourself: relationships you've gone quiet on, projects that have stalled, topics that keep coming up without resolution.

## What's different about it

Most AI assistants live in a chat window and you copy-paste context into them. Fluso connects to the tools where the context already is, so it starts every conversation knowing more about your work than a general-purpose chatbot can.

It also acts. A chatbot tells you what to do. Fluso drafts the email, schedules the meeting, opens the PR, posts the Slack update. Anything that touches another person — sending, posting, pushing — waits for your explicit approval. You see the action and decide. Reading and analysis happen automatically; writes don't.

## Who it's for

Founders and executives who lose two hours a day to inbox and meeting overhead. Developers who'd rather not stop coding to triage PRs. PMs who synthesise things for a living. Researchers who Google for hours and then format what they found. Teams that need shared context without yet another sync. Anyone who creates content from a description more than once a week.
