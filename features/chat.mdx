The chat is the front door. Everything Fluso does happens because you said something to it. The window looks like a messenger. It isn't one.

## What happens when you send a message

There's a small pause between hitting enter and seeing the first words come back. In that pause, Fluso gathers everything it has on the situation: this thread's history, your project's memory, your connected apps, the skills available, the files in scope, and your preferences. Then it picks tools and uses them. Sometimes one. Sometimes ten in sequence.

Ask it to summarise your unread emails and create tasks from the actionable ones. Behind the scenes: it queries Gmail, finds twelve unread, reads the five that look like requests, drafts three tasks with deadlines, drops them into your task list, and writes you a short summary that mentions all of it. You see the summary. The work is already done.

Describe the outcome. Get the outcome. Review.

## Asking for things well

Three things matter more than anything else.

**Specifics beat generalities.** "Help with email" isn't a request. "Summarise my unread emails and tell me which need a reply today" is. Fluso will do both — but the first comes back with a clarifying question, the second with a list.

**Outcomes, not steps.** You don't need to instruct Fluso the way you'd instruct a junior. "Search Gmail for messages from Sarah, read them, then tell me what she wants" is what a robot would type. "What did Sarah email me about?" is what you'd actually say. The second works fine.

**Steer mid-stream.** Replies stream token by token. If you can see a reply going somewhere wrong, type into the box: *"actually, just today"*, *"shorter"*, *"include the attachments"*. Fluso adjusts. You don't have to wait for it to finish a wrong answer.

## Threads

A thread is a conversation. It remembers what you've said and what Fluso has said back. That's why follow-ups work. After Fluso summarises your inbox, *"reply to the one from Legal"* makes sense because the inbox summary is in the context.

Start a new thread when you switch topic. The plus button at the top works, or you can ask for one in chat. Old threads stay in the sidebar. Pick any of them back up and Fluso has the full history.

Three slash commands that occasionally help: `/status` to see what Fluso is currently doing, `/schedules` to see what's queued, `/abort` to stop the current run. Most days you can forget they exist.

## Things you can do inside chat

Attach a file. Drag a PDF in and ask for a summary. Drop in an image and ask what's in the chart. Hand it a CSV and tell it to make a deck from the data. Files Fluso creates for you appear as attachments in the reply.

Work with files in your workspace: read a report, write a markdown file, append to an existing one.

Run commands: install a project's dependencies, run the test suite, check disk usage. None of this requires leaving the chat.

Use any connected app as part of an answer: pull the latest message from Sarah, search for a Slack thread, list open PRs that need review.

## A small etiquette

Anything that touches another person waits for your approval. Drafts of email and Slack messages get shown before they go. Pull requests get shown before they're filed. You read the diff, you click send. This is non-negotiable, and it's a feature: the speed of Fluso plus the judgment of you.

<Warning>
  Read external-facing drafts before approving. Fluso has good instincts. You have context Fluso doesn't.
</Warning>

A few habits that pay off:

One topic per thread. Switching subjects mid-thread blurs the context Fluso is using. The cost of starting a new thread is zero.

Switch into a project when you're working on something specific. Project context tightens the answers; without it, Fluso draws on everything.

Don't fight clarifying questions. If Fluso asks "Which project?" or "What format?", answer it. The back-and-forth is faster than guessing on its behalf.
