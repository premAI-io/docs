The interesting thing about Fluso's task list isn't that it exists. Most apps have one. The interesting thing is that you almost never type into it. Tasks turn up because Fluso saw them in your inbox, in a meeting transcript, or in a Slack thread, and pulled them in for you.

You spend your time on the work. The list keeps track of itself.

## Where tasks come from

The CFO writes: *"Can you have the Q2 numbers ready for the board meeting Friday? Also Sarah from Legal needs sign-off on the vendor contract by EOD Thursday."* By the time you've put down your coffee, two tasks exist:

- *Prepare Q2 numbers for board meeting* — high priority, due Friday, source: the CFO's email.
- *Sign off on vendor contract* — high priority, due Thursday, source: same email.

Both link back to the original message. You didn't open the inbox. You didn't type in a tracker. They're just there.

Meeting transcripts work the same way. When you say *"process this meeting transcript"*, every action item becomes a task. *"I'll follow up with Legal about the contract"* becomes a task on you. *"Sarah will own the redesign and have designs by next Friday"* becomes a task on Sarah, due that day. *"We need to decide on the Q3 budget by the 20th"* becomes a task with a hard deadline.

Slack picks up the same way if you point Fluso at it:

> *"Find any action items from #engineering this week and create tasks."*

And of course you can type one in directly when nobody's asked you to do it but you want to remember:

> *"Remind me to submit the expense report by Monday."*

> *"Add a task: update the onboarding docs with the new API changes, due next sprint."*

The mix in practice is roughly 80 percent auto, 20 percent manual.

## Categories and priority

Fluso categorises tasks as it creates them. Work, Emails, Personal, Finance, Travel, Health, Education, Shopping. The point is that you can focus on one bucket without the others crowding your view: *"What's left on my work tasks today?"*, *"What's still open on my finance list?"*

Priority is inferred from tone in the source message. "URGENT" or "by EOD" lands as urgent. "By Friday" or "this week" — or anything from your manager — lands as high. General requests without urgency cues are medium. "When you get a chance" or "no rush" is low. It gets it right most of the time and you can override anything.

## Scheduled tasks

Tasks with a due date get folded into the calendar view. Ask:

> *"Show me my day."*

You get back something like: standup at 9, then a 90-minute block to prepare the Q2 report (high, due Friday), then your 1:1 with Lisa, then a slot for PR #142, then the client call, then focus time, then catching up on vendor email. Calendar events and tasks, side by side, in one view. You don't have to open a separate app.

## Looking at the list

There are a few standard cuts of the list — *Attention needed* (overdue and urgent), *Today*, *This week*, by project, by category, completed. You navigate them in the sidebar or just ask:

> *"Show me high-priority tasks due this week."*

> *"What tasks are assigned to Sarah?"*

> *"Find tasks about the product launch."*

> *"Show completed tasks from last month."*

A task you click into shows you everything: title, source (with a link to the email or transcript), due date, priority, category, the relevant context Fluso pulled out (e.g., *"CFO says: Sarah from Legal needs sign-off on the vendor contract by EOD Thursday"*), related items, and the change history (created when, priority bumped when, by whom).

The history is more useful than it sounds. Friday review: *"What did I complete this week?"* Status update: *"Where do the Acme deliverables stand?"* Memory check: *"Why did we deprioritise this?"* All of those work because every change was logged.

## Updating

Updates are conversational:

> *"Mark the vendor contract task as done."*

> *"Make the Q2 report task urgent. Board moved up."*

> *"Move the presentation to next week."*

> *"Assign the design review to Sarah."*

Bulk operations work the same way:

> *"Reschedule all overdue tasks to tomorrow."*

> *"Mark all newsletter-related tasks as low priority."*

> *"Archive completed tasks from the Q1 project."*

## Projects, recurring tasks, reminders

Tasks belong to projects when context demands it. *"Show me all tasks for the Q2 Planning project"* scopes the view. *"Add a task to the website redesign project: update the hero copy"* scopes the creation.

Recurring tasks are a single sentence:

> *"Remind me every Monday to review weekly metrics."*

> *"Recurring task: submit expense reports every other Friday."*

Notifications come through whichever channel you've connected — in-app, email, or Slack — when something approaches its deadline.

## A small habit that makes the rest worthwhile

Spend two minutes each morning reviewing the auto-created tasks. Adjust the priorities Fluso got slightly wrong. Kill anything that isn't actually a task. Mark the ones you knocked out yesterday as done. After that, the list is right for the day, and you can stop thinking about it.
