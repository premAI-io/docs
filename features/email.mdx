Connect Gmail and Fluso starts handling the parts of email that don't really need you. Triage. Summaries of long threads. Drafts. Pulling out the things people are asking you to do and turning them into tasks. The mail itself still arrives in Gmail; Fluso just stops you from having to read most of it directly.

## Triage

Mornings are the obvious starting point. Forty-seven unread, you haven't looked since yesterday.

> *"Summarise my unread emails from today."*

You get back: three need action (Sarah's contract review by Friday, the CFO's Q2 numbers for the board, an urgent production hotfix); five are FYI (weekly metrics, a roadmap update, two newsletters worth glancing at, a vendor announcement); thirty-nine are noise (notification digests, marketing, automated alerts). Tasks for the action items are already created. Drafts for the two replies that need writing are ready.

Five minutes. Forty-seven emails handled.

The same shape works at sharper grain:

> *"Which emails need a reply today?"*

> *"Show me only urgent emails."*

> *"Anything from my boss I haven't responded to?"*

> *"What emails mention the Acme deal?"*

## Summaries

A long thread is a thing Fluso reads better than you do.

> *"Summarise the email thread about the vendor negotiation."*

You get who said what, what was decided, where it's stuck, and what's pending. Single emails work the same way. *"Summarise the latest email from Legal"* gives you the ask, the deadline, and what you owe back.

The most useful version is across messages, not within one:

> *"Summarise all emails from last week about the product launch."*

Fluso finds them itself, groups them, and gives you one synthesis instead of fifteen messages.

## Search

Gmail's search is powerful and almost nobody uses it. Fluso uses it for you, in plain English:

> *"Find the email where Thomas sent the pricing spreadsheet."*

> *"Emails from @acme.com in the last two weeks that mention 'contract' and have attachments."*

> *"Unread mail older than three days."*

> *"Starred emails from April about the budget."*

Fluso translates the request into a Gmail query and returns the results. If you're vague, it asks. If you're specific, it finds them.

## Replies

Drafts are the part most people take a beat to trust, and reasonably so. A few patterns work well.

The simplest is direct:

> *"Reply to Sarah's email. I've reviewed the proposal and have three changes. I'll send detailed feedback by Thursday."*

You can hand off the gist and let Fluso shape the prose:

> *"Reply briefly to the team. Sounds good, let's discuss in tomorrow's standup."*

You can iterate after you see the draft:

> *"Make it more formal."* / *"Softer, this is sensitive."* / *"Shorter, just the key points."*

You can compose from scratch:

> *"Write an email to engineering announcing the code freeze and what it means for deployments."*

<Warning>
  Drafts always wait for your approval. Fluso doesn't send anything until you've read it and clicked confirm. Even so: read sensitive or external-facing replies carefully before approving.
</Warning>

## Auto-tasks

This is the feature most people stop noticing because it works. Fluso scans your inbox in the background and turns messages that ask you to do something into tracked tasks. Title, deadline if mentioned, priority based on tone, link back to the source. The tasks themselves live in [Tasks](/features/tasks).

The CFO writes "Can you have the Q2 numbers ready for the board meeting Friday? Also Sarah from Legal needs sign-off on the vendor contract by EOD Thursday." Two tasks appear in your list before you've opened the email: *Prepare Q2 numbers for board meeting* (high, due Friday) and *Sign off on vendor contract* (high, due Thursday). Both linked back to the source.

What gets skipped: newsletters, marketing, "thanks", FYI updates that don't ask for anything, calendar invites (those go through the calendar integration). The miss rate is low after a couple of days. Before then, you may need to mark a few messages as not actually tasks. It learns.

You can adjust priorities, deadlines, or kill misclassifications. The first morning's task list might need ten seconds of cleanup; the third morning's usually doesn't.

## Follow-ups

Fluso remembers when you sent something that wanted a response and tracks whether one came back:

> *"Has the client responded about the contract?"*

> *"Did Legal reply to my questions from Monday?"*

> *"Anyone I emailed last week who hasn't responded?"*

If the answer is no and the silence is awkward, you can ask for a polite nudge and approve it.

## Patterns that pay off

A morning brief instead of opening Gmail. *"Morning brief"* combines the email summary, today's calendar, tasks due, and follow-ups. Two minutes start to finish.

A weekly digest on Friday. *"Give me a weekly summary — what happened in my email this week?"* surfaces decisions, pending items, and people you've gone quiet on.

A project-scoped sweep when something's been simmering. *"Find all emails about the Acme project and tell me where things stand."* Even months of mail get groomed into a status update.
