Fluso doesn't forget. Every email, every meeting transcript, every Slack thread, every doc you create — they all feed into a connected map of your work. People. Projects. Decisions. The companies you talk to and the topics you keep coming back to. It's running in the background and you mostly don't think about it.

The reason it matters is the question you can ask three months later: *"What do I know about Company X?"* The answer is the actual answer, not your hazy recollection of one.

## What's in there

People you interact with end up with a profile. Sarah Chen, VP Product at Acme, sarah@acme.com. First contact in March at the conference. Twelve emails, three meetings, two Slack mentions since. Topics: partnership, API integration, Q3 budget, pilot. Open with you: contract awaiting Legal review, your action item. Style: direct, data-driven, prefers async follow-ups.

You didn't write any of that. Fluso built it from the traffic.

Projects get the same treatment. The Acme project's memory has the documents shared, the meeting notes and decisions, the people involved with their roles, the timeline of milestones, the open questions, what's blocked and on whom. Asking *"What's the status of the Acme project?"* gets you a real status: last activity Sarah's email on May 8 about pilot expansion, next milestone the contract signing on May 20, blocked on Legal review (3 days overdue), team is you plus James and Lisa, the key decision agreed April 15 was the Q3 scope.

Organisations and topics get tracked the same way — who's at which company, which projects connect to which clients, which competitors you've discussed and when, which technologies keep coming up.

## How it builds

Without you doing anything. There's no tagging, no foldering, no "save to memory" button. An email from sarah@acme.com gets linked to Sarah's profile and the Acme project. A meeting transcript gets parsed for decisions and action items. A Slack discussion about a competitor gets connected to the last time that competitor came up.

The graph is connected, not siloed. A person isn't just a contact — they're also a meeting attendee, a Slack handle, a doc collaborator. A project isn't just a folder — it spans email threads, meetings, conversations, and files. So when you ask "what do I know about the pricing discussion?", Fluso doesn't search one source. It pulls from all of them.

You don't browse it. You ask it questions:

> *"What did we decide about the Q3 budget?"*

> *"When did I last talk to the Stripe team?"*

> *"Who from Deloitte was at the cybersecurity meeting?"*

> *"What have we discussed about AI regulation?"*

## What you do with it

Recall is the obvious one:

> *"What's the history of our relationship with Acme Corp?"*

A timeline comes back. First contact, key meetings, decisions made, current status, open items. From email, meetings, and Slack together.

Meeting prep is recall in tighter form:

> *"Brief me on my 3pm with Sarah."*

You get her role and background, your communication history, last meeting topics, open action items, recent emails, related Slack discussions, and anything she's shared with you. The thing you'd otherwise spend half an hour assembling.

Decision recovery is the one that surprises people:

> *"What did we decide about the product roadmap in the April planning meeting?"*

Even if it was six weeks ago and you didn't take notes, the answer is there because you uploaded the transcript at the time.

There's also the find-who-knows-what version: *"Who on the team has worked on the authentication system?"*, *"Who's been in client meetings with Acme?"*

The relationship-health version: *"How's the Acme relationship going? Any red flags?"* Fluso surfaces patterns that are easy to miss yourself — slower response times, fewer meetings, terser emails.

The connection-finding version: *"Is there any connection between the Acme deal and the Stripe conversation?"* Same person on both threads, same project mentioned in two contexts, both surfaced in the same week. Connections you wouldn't bother to look for.

## Privacy and control

The graph is yours. It lives in your isolated workspace. No other user sees it. Nothing leaves it for third parties. Fluso doesn't train models on it.

You can delete any entry, or wipe the whole thing, at any time. The graph only ever builds from apps you've connected — disconnect Slack and Slack data stops contributing.

For things you don't want tracked, you say so:

> *"Don't track anything related to Project X."*

> *"Forget everything about this vendor."*

## The shape of the value over time

In the first week, the graph knows your frequent contacts and current projects. Recent things are summarisable; older context is thin.

By the end of the first month, the history of key relationships is in there, project status is real-time, your communication patterns are visible. Meeting prep starts feeling genuinely sharp.

Three months in, Fluso starts surfacing things you wouldn't have thought to ask: relationships you've gone quiet on, projects that have stalled, topics that keep resurfacing without resolution.

After six months, it's institutional memory. *"What was the outcome of that vendor negotiation in January?"* gets a direct answer, with citations.

The graph doesn't feel transformative on day three. By week six it's quietly indispensable.

<Tip>
  The most common mistake is judging memory after a few days. It's subtle until it isn't. Use Fluso normally for a couple of weeks before deciding what it can or can't recall — the difference between week one and week three is large.
</Tip>
