Fluso doesn't forget. Every email, meeting, Slack thread, and document feeds a connected map of your work life. The more you use it, the more context Fluso has — and the more useful it becomes.

## What Fluso remembers

### People

Fluso builds profiles for everyone you interact with — automatically. You don't fill anything in.

<Card title="Sample profile">
**Sarah Chen**
- Role: VP Product, Acme Corp
- Email: sarah@acme.com
- First contact: March 15, 2026 (conference introduction)
- Last contact: May 8, 2026 (email about pilot expansion)
- Communication: 12 emails, 3 meetings, 2 Slack mentions
- Topics: partnership, API integration, Q3 budget, pilot
- Open items: contract awaiting Legal review (your action item)
- Style: direct, data-driven, prefers async follow-ups
</Card>

### Projects

Each project has a rich memory:

- Documents and files shared.
- Meeting notes and decisions.
- People involved and their roles.
- Timeline of milestones and deadlines.
- Open questions and blocked items.
- Stage in the lifecycle.

> *"What's the status of the Acme project?"*

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Last activity | Email from Sarah on May 8 about pilot expansion |
| Next milestone | Contract signing — target May 20 |
| Blocked on | Legal review (3 days overdue) |
| Team | You, James (Eng), Lisa (Product) |
| Key decisions | Q3 scope agreed on April 15 |

### Organisations

Fluso maps companies and teams: who works where, what projects you have with them, how communication flows, the history of the relationship.

### Topics

What you discuss across time — technologies, competitors, industry trends, internal initiatives.

## How the knowledge graph works

### Automatic, not manual

You never tag, categorise, or file anything. Fluso watches your activity and connects:

- An email from `sarah@acme.com` → linked to Sarah's profile → linked to the Acme project → tagged with topics mentioned.
- A meeting about Q2 metrics → linked to the Q2 Planning project → action items extracted → decisions recorded.
- A Slack discussion about a competitor → linked to your competitive intelligence → connected to past discussions about the same competitor.

### Connected, not siloed

A person isn't just an email contact — they're also a meeting attendee, a Slack mention, a document collaborator. A project isn't just a folder — it spans emails, meetings, documents, and conversations.

When you ask *"What do I know about the pricing discussion?"*, Fluso finds relevant information across **all** sources, not just one.

### Searchable, not browsable

You don't navigate folders or tags. You ask:

> *"What did we decide about the Q3 budget?"*

> *"When did I last talk to the Stripe team?"*

> *"Who from Deloitte was at the cybersecurity meeting?"*

> *"What have we discussed about AI regulation?"*

> *"Find everything related to the server migration last month."*

## What you can do with the knowledge graph

### Recall anything instantly

> *"What's the history of our relationship with Acme Corp?"*

Fluso gives you a timeline: first contact, key meetings, decisions, current status, open items — across emails, meetings, and Slack.

### Prepare for meetings with full context

> *"Brief me on my 3pm with Sarah."*

Role and background, communication history, last meeting topics, open action items, recent emails, related Slack discussions, documents shared.

### Never lose a decision

> *"What did we decide about the product roadmap in the April planning meeting?"*

Even if it was six weeks ago and you didn't take notes, Fluso knows — because you uploaded the transcript.

### Find who knows what

> *"Who on the team has worked on the authentication system?"*

> *"Who's been in the client meetings with Acme?"*

> *"Who discussed the pricing changes in Slack last month?"*

### Track relationship health

> *"How's the Acme relationship going? Any red flags?"*

Fluso surfaces patterns: longer response times, fewer meetings, terser emails — early warning signs you'd miss.

### Discover connections

> *"Is there any connection between the Acme deal and the Stripe conversation?"*

Fluso finds the thread: same person on both emails, same project referenced, both discussions in the same week — connections you'd never find manually.

## Privacy and control

### Your data is yours

- Your knowledge graph is stored in your isolated workspace.
- No other user can access it.
- Nothing is shared with third parties.
- You can delete any entry at any time.
- You can delete the entire graph.

### You control what's tracked

Fluso only builds knowledge from apps you've explicitly connected. If Slack isn't connected, no Slack data enters the graph. Disconnect an app and that source stops contributing.

### Sensitive information

If something shouldn't be tracked, just say:

> *"Don't track anything related to Project X."*

> *"Forget everything about this vendor."*

## The compound effect

The knowledge graph grows more valuable over time:

| Time | What you get |
|---|---|
| **Week 1** | Frequent contacts, current projects, recent email and meeting summaries. |
| **Month 1** | History of key relationships, project status, your communication patterns. Meeting prep is genuinely useful. |
| **Month 3** | Patterns you wouldn't see yourself — neglected relationships, stalling projects, recurring topics without resolution. |
| **Month 6+** | Institutional memory. *"What was the outcome of that vendor negotiation in January?"* — answered instantly. |

<Tip>
  **Trust the process.** The graph feels magical after a few weeks but subtle in the first few days. Give it time to accumulate. The value compounds.
</Tip>

<Info>
  Your knowledge graph is built on top of your connected apps. The more apps you connect — Gmail, Calendar, Slack, GitHub — the richer and more useful it becomes.
</Info>
