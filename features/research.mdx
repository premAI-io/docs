The research feature exists for a specific frustration: you ask a question, end up with fifteen browser tabs, and an hour goes. Fluso turns the question into a structured answer with citations.

It does the obvious version (deep research with sources) and a few less obvious ones (video summaries, statistical analysis, data quality audits).

## Deep research

You ask. Fluso searches the web, reads the most relevant sources, cross-references claims across them, structures the findings, and cites every claim with a link.

There are three depths, depending on what you actually need.

A quick lookup — *"What's NVIDIA's current market cap?"* — comes back as a sentence in seconds. No report, no scaffolding, just the answer.

A standard report — *"Compare the top three CRM platforms for small business"* — takes a couple of minutes and produces a short cited document with the key findings.

A deep report — *"Analyse the enterprise AI agent market. Players, pricing, trends, M&A activity"* — takes 5 to 10 minutes and produces a structured document with executive summary, key findings, detailed analysis section by section, comparison tables where they help, recommendations, and 15 to 30 sources.

The categories Fluso is good at:

- Market analysis (TAM, players, segmentation).
- Competitive intel (a specific company's product, pricing, recent funding, position).
- Technology comparisons (vector databases, web frameworks, hosting providers).
- Regulation (the EU AI Act and SaaS, deadlines, compliance).
- Company research (everything-they-have on a specific firm).
- Trend analysis (where adoption is moving in a sector).

A request like *"top five enterprise AI platforms compared on pricing, features, target market, and funding, with citations"* produces a sharper report than *"research AI platforms"*. Specificity drives quality, the same way it does in any request you'd make of a research analyst.

## Video summaries

Paste a YouTube link, get back what the video said. Useful when someone insists you watch a 90-minute keynote and you have ten minutes.

> *"Summarise this YouTube video: [link]"*

You get an executive summary in three to five paragraphs, the topics organised by theme, every important moment with a timestamp link you can click into the video, the notable quotes from the speaker, and what to do with the information.

Every summary is saved. Weeks later: *"What did that keynote say about prompt engineering?"* — Fluso looks in your library and answers.

Works on YouTube of any length, conference talks, tutorials, product demos, video podcasts. Non-English videos work with a language hint.

## Data analysis

The statistical version is for when you're staring at A/B test numbers and want to know if you should ship.

> *"Our A/B test ran for two weeks. Control: 1,000 visitors, 50 conversions. Variant: 1,000 visitors, 65 conversions. Is this significant? What's the effect size? Should we ship it?"*

You get back a real answer with the test (two-proportion z-test), result (statistically significant, p = 0.042), effect size (Cohen's h = 0.15, small to medium), confidence interval (95% CI [0.3%, 2.7%]), and a plain-English read: the variant likely improves conversion by 1.5 percentage points, from 5.0% to 6.5%, real but modest. Recommendation: ship, monitor for two more weeks to confirm.

The kinds of analysis Fluso runs: A/B tests, survey responses, before/after comparisons, correlation, sample-size calculations.

The data-quality version is for when you have a dataset and don't fully trust it:

> *"Audit this dataset before we use it. Any quality issues?"*

You get back a prioritised list — missing values and where they're concentrated, inconsistencies in format, outliers that don't make sense, validity issues (wrong types, impossible values), duplicates and near-duplicates — ranked by severity.

## Competitive intelligence

Worth calling out separately because it combines public research with your private knowledge graph:

> *"Research our top three competitors. Pricing, features, recent moves, and where they're vulnerable."*

What comes back layers two things. The public picture: web research, news, funding data. The private picture: what you've discussed about them in emails and Slack, what's come up in meetings. The combination is sharper than either alone. You get the context only in your head plus the context only on the internet, in one report.

## Pairing research with content

The most useful pattern is asking for the artifact, not the research:

> *"Research the state of AI in healthcare and create a 15-slide presentation with citations."*

> *"Analyse this market data and create a PDF report with charts and recommendations."*

> *"Research competitor pricing and build a comparison spreadsheet."*

You skip the step where you'd take the research output and reformat it. Fluso just builds the deliverable.

<Warning>
  Research is based on what's publicly available at the time of the query. Things change. Citations are linked, so for high-stakes decisions, click through and verify the load-bearing claims yourself.
