The point of connecting your calendar is what happens around the meetings, not the calendar itself. Before a meeting Fluso preps you. During the meeting you focus. After, the transcript becomes a summary, decisions, tasks, and a follow-up email — automatically.

## The basics

You can ask the obvious things:

> *"What's on my calendar today?"*

> *"What does my week look like?"*

> *"Show me my meetings with Acme this month."*

Replies aren't bare event lists. For each meeting Fluso adds context: who's there, what you've discussed before, what's pending. The basic schedule check turns into a brief.

Finding time is conversational:

> *"When am I free for thirty minutes tomorrow?"*

> *"Find a one-hour slot this week when the engineering team is available."*

> *"I need two hours of focus time today. Where can I block it?"*

Fluso looks at your calendars (including shared ones), accounts for tasks already pinned to time, and proposes options that actually work — not slots that overlap with the deep-work block you put on Thursday.

Scheduling works the same way:

> *"Schedule a thirty-minute standup tomorrow at 9am."*

> *"Create a meeting with Sarah next Monday at 2pm about the Q3 review."*

> *"Block 3-5pm today for focused work."*

> *"Set up a recurring 1:1 with my direct reports every other Tuesday."*

## Meeting prep

The single highest-leverage thing the calendar integration does. Before a meeting, ask:

> *"Prep me for my 3pm."*

You get a brief that pulls from everywhere: who's attending and what you know about them, the last time you met and what you discussed, recent emails that touch the topic, Slack threads that mention it, the open action items between the two of you, and a guess at what's likely to come up based on recent traffic.

A real one looks roughly like this. The 3pm with Sarah and Mark from Acme: VP Product and CTO respectively, you last met April 18 and agreed on Phase 1 scope, contract pending Legal. Since then there have been three emails from Sarah about pilot expansion and a Slack discussion in #partnerships about Acme's Q3 budget. Open items: Legal review (overdue, your action) and technical specs (Friday, James's). Likely topics: pilot expansion, API rate limiting (their team has been asking in Slack), Q3 pricing.

You walk in informed without spending half an hour scrambling.

## Meeting intelligence

Upload a transcript or a recording. Fluso handles the rest.

It accepts the usual formats — `.txt`, `.vtt`, `.srt`, `.docx`, `.md` for text, audio files for recordings. Transcripts from Zoom, Meet, Teams, Otter, Fireflies, Granola all just work.

Then:

> *"Process this meeting transcript."*

What comes back is everything you would have spent forty-five minutes producing yourself: an executive summary in three to five paragraphs, every decision as a bullet, action items as tasks (with assignees and deadlines if they were stated), a draft email summary for the attendees, a Slack update for the relevant channel, and the attendees and topics linked into your knowledge graph for next time.

A worked example. You upload a forty-five-minute product review. Fluso returns:

The team reviewed Q2 product metrics and discussed Q3. Revenue is up 12 percent, but retention dropped 3 percent. Q3 will prioritise retention over new features. Three features are descoped: automated reporting, dark mode, and the mobile widget.

Decisions: retention over new features for Q3; automated reporting moves to Q4; dark mode and mobile widget are off the roadmap; new UX researcher approved to start July 1.

Tasks: Lisa drafts the retention plan by May 20. James audits the onboarding flow for friction by May 18. You update the roadmap doc by May 17. HR sends the offer letter by May 15. Lisa notifies customers about descoped features by May 22.

The follow-up email and Slack update are ready to send.

That used to be forty-five minutes of cleanup. It's about a minute of review.

## Long-term analysis

Once Fluso has processed enough meetings, it can look at patterns across them:

> *"Analyse my meetings from the last two weeks."*

It looks at things you'd never count yourself — speaking ratio, filler-word frequency, who interrupts whom, whether your meetings end with concrete next steps or fade out, and how those numbers are moving over time.

A real one sounds like: *Speaking ratio 42 percent, balanced. Filler words 8 per minute, above average; the "pause instead of um" technique helps. You interrupted others four times across six meetings; you were interrupted twice. Filler words are down from 12 to 8 per minute over the last month and your speaking ratio has come down from 55 to 42 percent. Recommendation: keep working on filler words; otherwise effective.*

This is private to you. It's diagnostic, not graded.

## A few useful patterns

The full meeting workflow becomes one minute before, one minute after, plus the meeting itself: prep with one prompt, process the transcript with one prompt, approve drafts. See [End-to-end meetings](/use-cases/meetings) for the worked version.

A meeting-free day takes one prompt: *"Block off Thursday for deep work. Decline or reschedule any non-urgent meetings."*

A recurring-meeting audit catches the ones that should be emails: *"Review my recurring meetings. Which have low attendance or could be async?"*

<Warning>
  Always tell the people in a meeting that you're recording it. Follow your organisation's policies on recording and transcription. The technology is fine; the etiquette and the law matter.
</Warning>
